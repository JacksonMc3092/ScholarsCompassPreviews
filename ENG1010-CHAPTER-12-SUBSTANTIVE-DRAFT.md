# Chapter 12: Finding and Evaluating Sources

## From Search Results to Useful Evidence

Research can begin with a deceptively simple moment: you open a search box, type your entire essay question, and receive thousands or millions of results. Somewhere in that avalanche may be exactly what you need. There may also be advertisements, repeated summaries of the same claim, outdated material, sources written for a different audience, and pages that look polished while saying very little.

The challenge is not merely finding information. The challenge is making good decisions about where to search, what to trust, what to keep, and what each source will actually do in your paper.

A research paper does not become strong because it contains a large pile of sources. It becomes strong when the writer chooses sources deliberately, understands them accurately, and gives each one a clear job.

> **Key Insight:** A source belongs in the paper because it has a job, not because it was difficult to find.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- turn a broad topic into a focused research question and useful search terms;
- choose among library databases, Google Scholar, and the open web;
- revise a search when the first results are weak, overwhelming, or repetitive;
- use SIFT and lateral reading to investigate online information;
- distinguish a source’s credibility from its usefulness;
- match different source types to different research needs;
- keep a source log that records both citation information and a plan for using the source;
- save the information needed for accurate MLA citations while you research.

## I. Research Is a Route, Not a Single Search Box

A broad topic names the territory. A research question gives you a direction through that territory. Keywords create the routes you can try.

Suppose your topic is **social media and college students**. That topic is real, but it is too large to guide a focused search. Social media could involve attention, sleep, friendship, political information, advertising, identity, privacy, body image, career networking, or dozens of other issues.

A stronger research question might be:

> **How does late-night use of short-form video platforms affect the sleep routines and academic habits of first-year college students?**

That question gives the research a shape. It identifies:

- a behavior: late-night use;
- a platform type: short-form video;
- possible effects: sleep routines and academic habits;
- a population: first-year college students.

You probably should not paste the entire question into every search box. Instead, break it into concepts and build a keyword map.

### Example: Topic-to-Keyword Map

| Main concept | Related terms and alternatives |
|---|---|
| short-form video | TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, algorithmic video feed |
| late-night use | bedtime use, nighttime screen use, evening media use, screen time before sleep |
| sleep | sleep quality, sleep duration, bedtime delay, insomnia, circadian rhythm |
| academic habits | study habits, attention, class attendance, academic performance, procrastination |
| first-year college students | freshmen, first-year undergraduates, college students, emerging adults |

A first search might combine only two or three concepts:

- `TikTok AND sleep quality AND college students`
- `short-form video AND bedtime delay`
- `nighttime screen use AND academic performance`
- `social media AND sleep AND first-year students`

Different words open different doors. Researchers may use a technical phrase you did not know when you started. A promising article may introduce the term **bedtime procrastination**, which then becomes a useful search term. Research is partly the process of learning the language other people use to discuss your question.

> **Toolkit: Build Better Keywords**
>
> 1. Write your research question.
> 2. Circle the two or three most important concepts.
> 3. List synonyms, narrower terms, broader terms, names, places, institutions, and dates connected to each concept.
> 4. Try several combinations.
> 5. Record the terms that produce useful results.

Your first search is not a final exam. It is a scouting trip.

## II. The Three-Lane Search Method

Strong research often moves among three search lanes: **library databases**, **Google Scholar**, and **the open web**. These lanes overlap, but they are not interchangeable. Each is built for different kinds of discovery.

### Lane 1: Library Databases

Library databases collect and organize material that the college provides through subscriptions. Depending on the database, that material may include scholarly journal articles, newspapers, magazines, reference entries, reports, ebooks, statistics, primary documents, and professional publications.

The CT State Library provides access to multidisciplinary and subject-specific databases, research guides, citation support, and help from librarians. Database availability can change, so enter through the current CT State Library portal rather than relying on an old bookmarked vendor link.

Library databases are especially useful when you need:

- peer-reviewed scholarship;
- credible newspaper or magazine coverage that may sit behind a paywall on the open web;
- reliable background information;
- subject headings and filters that help narrow a topic;
- publication details and stable records for citation.

A database is not simply “better Google.” It is a structured collection. Search results often include an abstract, subject terms, publication type, date filters, and a clear record of where the material appeared.

> **Example:** A student researching hostile architecture might begin in a multidisciplinary database with `hostile architecture AND public space`. A useful article may introduce related terms such as **defensive design**, **exclusionary design**, or **anti-homeless design**. Those terms can improve later searches in all three lanes.

### Lane 2: Google Scholar

Google Scholar searches academic material across publishers, repositories, court opinions, books, theses, conference papers, and other scholarly locations. It is useful for entering an academic conversation and following the trails around a promising source.

Google Scholar can help you:

- search an exact article title in quotation marks;
- search for work by a particular author;
- limit results by year;
- follow **Cited by** to find newer work that refers to a source;
- use **Related articles** to find similar research;
- check **All versions** or a PDF/HTML link for an accessible copy;
- connect with library subscriptions when access is configured.

Google Scholar normally sorts results by relevance rather than simply placing the newest item first. That is useful, but it also means you should check dates and not assume the first result is automatically the best result for your paper.

A high citation count can tell you that other writers have referred to a work. It cannot tell you, by itself, whether the source is correct, current, appropriate for your question, or being cited positively. Citation counts are clues, not crowns.

### Lane 3: The Open Web

The open web is where you may find:

- government data and public records;
- current journalism;
- organizational reports;
- policies and laws;
- speeches, advertisements, posts, videos, and other primary sources;
- public statements from people or institutions you are studying;
- datasets, maps, images, and multimedia;
- information too recent to have reached a scholarly journal.

The web is not automatically less credible than a database. A federal agency’s original dataset may be more authoritative for a specific statistic than an article summarizing it. A company’s own advertisement may be the correct primary source if your paper analyzes that company’s rhetoric. A local newspaper may provide current reporting that a scholarly article published two years ago could not include.

The open web also demands more investigation because publication standards vary widely. A professional design, an official-sounding name, or a `.org` address does not prove that a page is reliable.

### The Lanes Work Together

Research rarely travels in a straight line.

A library article may give you the vocabulary for a better web search. A government report may name an expert whose scholarship you then locate in Google Scholar. A news article may summarize a study, leading you to trace the claim back to the original research. A database search may reveal that the “new” idea in a viral post has been debated for years.

> **Key Insight:** Choose a search lane based on what you need, not on a rule that one lane is always superior.

### Quick Route Chooser

| What you need | A strong place to begin |
|---|---|
| scholarly research on a focused question | library database or Google Scholar |
| current reporting on a recent event | newspaper database or credible open-web journalism |
| a law, policy, public record, or government statistic | official government source |
| background and vocabulary for an unfamiliar topic | reference database, research guide, or strong overview |
| an advertisement, speech, social post, or company statement to analyze | original primary source on the open web |
| professional practice within a field | professional or trade publication |
| multiple viewpoints on a public issue | database designed for issues and controversies, plus independent sources |

## III. Search Like a Writer, Not a Slot Machine

A weak search process repeats the same vague query and hopes the next page of results will somehow improve. A stronger process treats each search as information about what to try next.

### A Repeatable Search Cycle

1. **Search with two or three meaningful terms.** Begin broad enough to discover vocabulary but focused enough to avoid a result ocean.
2. **Scan before clicking.** Read titles, dates, publication names, authors, abstracts, and result descriptions. Do not automatically surrender your afternoon to the first blue link.
3. **Collect useful vocabulary.** Notice recurring terms, names, theories, locations, laws, organizations, and subject headings.
4. **Revise the search.** Add a narrower term, replace a weak term, try a synonym, remove a word, or change lanes.
5. **Record what worked.** Save strong search strings in your source log so you can return to them.

### Useful Search Moves

**Quotation marks** search for an exact phrase:

- `"bedtime procrastination"`
- `"hostile architecture"`

**AND** requires both concepts in many databases:

- `college students AND sleep quality`

**OR** searches for alternatives or synonyms:

- `TikTok OR "Instagram Reels" OR "YouTube Shorts"`

**Date filters** help when currency matters. A source on a historical event may remain useful decades later; a source about current platform features or public-health guidance may age much faster.

**Subject terms** are controlled labels used by many databases. Think of them as official tags. A strong result may list subject terms that lead to a more precise collection of material.

**References** lead backward to earlier sources. **Cited by** leads forward to newer work that refers to the source. **Related articles** moves sideways to similar research.

### When Search Results Are Too Broad

Add a population, place, time period, platform, discipline, or specific effect.

Broad:

- `social media effects`

Narrower:

- `short-form video AND sleep quality AND college students`

### When Search Results Are Too Narrow

Remove one concept, use a broader term, try a synonym, or search the references of the one useful source you found.

Too narrow:

- `first-semester community college students TikTok bedtime procrastination Connecticut`

Broader:

- `college students social media bedtime procrastination`

### When You Find Only Sources That Repeat One Another

Change the job you are searching for. Instead of asking for more sources that agree with your first source, search for:

- limitations;
- criticism;
- a different population;
- an alternative explanation;
- a historical comparison;
- a primary source;
- a systematic review;
- a counterargument;
- the original dataset or study.

Research should deepen the conversation, not build a hall of mirrors.

## IV. SIFT: Pause Before You Trust, Use, or Share

SIFT is a practical method associated with digital-literacy educator Mike Caulfield. The letters name four moves:

1. **Stop**
2. **Investigate the source**
3. **Find better coverage**
4. **Trace claims, quotations, and media to the original context**

SIFT is especially useful when you encounter a claim online and need to decide what deserves your attention.

### S: Stop

Before reading every word or sharing the page, pause.

Ask:

- Do I recognize this source?
- What do I already know about its reputation?
- What claim is being made?
- What is my purpose? Am I looking for background, evidence, a primary source, or something else?
- Is the claim emotionally charged in a way that makes me eager to believe or reject it?

Stopping does not mean becoming suspicious of everything. It means refusing to let the page set the pace of your judgment.

### I: Investigate the Source

Find out who is behind the information.

Ask:

- Who is the author or organization?
- What relevant expertise or experience do they have?
- Where was the material published?
- What is the publication’s purpose, ownership, or editorial process?
- Is the author reporting original work, summarizing someone else, selling something, advocating for a position, or entertaining an audience?

Do not rely entirely on the source’s own “About” page. An organization is unlikely to describe itself as “a beautifully designed fog machine.” Look for independent descriptions.

### F: Find Better Coverage

Search for other credible reporting, research, or analysis of the same claim.

The goal is not always to find a source that says the opposite. The goal is to understand how the claim fits into a wider body of information.

Ask:

- Do other reliable sources report the same thing?
- Is there stronger, clearer, or more complete coverage?
- Is there a source with greater expertise or closer access to the evidence?
- Are important limitations missing from the version I first saw?

Sometimes your original source is adequate. Sometimes better coverage makes the original source unnecessary.

### T: Trace to the Original Context

Online claims often travel through several layers. A post summarizes a news story, which summarizes a press release, which summarizes a study. At each step, context can shrink and certainty can inflate.

Trace:

- a statistic to the dataset or report;
- a quotation to the full interview, speech, article, or transcript;
- a scientific claim to the original study;
- an image to its earlier use and caption;
- a policy claim to the actual law, rule, or official document.

The original source is not automatically perfect, but it lets you see what was actually measured, said, shown, or decided.

### SIFT Walkthrough: “Handwritten Notes Are Always Better”

Imagine that a viral post says:

> “A scientific study proves that students who take handwritten notes always learn more than students who use laptops.”

**Stop:** The word **always** is unusually absolute. The post does not name the study, students, subject, or type of learning.

**Investigate:** The account posting the claim sells notebooks and study planners. That does not automatically make the claim false, but it gives the account a commercial purpose and no obvious research expertise.

**Find better coverage:** Search for independent reporting, scholarly reviews, and later studies on handwritten and digital note-taking. Look for differences in task, student population, distraction, typing style, accessibility, and whether students reviewed their notes afterward.

**Trace:** Follow the post’s link to a blog, the blog’s link to a news article, and the news article’s link to the original study. Read the abstract, methods, and limitations. The study may compare one kind of laptop note-taking with one kind of handwriting task under specific conditions. That is valuable evidence, but it is not the same as proving an eternal law for every learner and every class.

The SIFT process does not require you to “debunk” the claim. It helps you replace an inflated claim with a more accurate understanding.

> **Key Insight:** Tracing a claim often changes the question from “Is this true?” to “Exactly what does the evidence support?”

## V. Lateral Reading: Leave the Page to Understand the Page

Close reading stays inside a text. You examine the writer’s words, evidence, organization, tone, and rhetorical choices.

Lateral reading temporarily leaves the page. You open other tabs to investigate the author, publication, organization, evidence, and reputation from the outside.

Professional fact-checkers often resist spending too much time on an unfamiliar page before learning who is behind it. A beautifully organized article can consume twenty minutes before you discover that the “research institute” publishing it is a marketing project run by one person.

### A Short Lateral-Reading Routine

1. Open a new tab.
2. Search the author’s name, organization, and publication.
3. Read independent descriptions, not only the source’s biography or About page.
4. Look for relevant expertise, ownership, funding, editorial standards, corrections, and past work.
5. Search the central claim separately.
6. Return to the original page with better context.

### What About Bias?

“Bias” is often treated as a trapdoor word: once someone spots a perspective, the source falls through the floor. That is too simple.

Every source is created by someone, for some purpose, from some position. A government agency, advocacy group, newspaper, corporation, professor, community organization, and student journalist all have purposes and limits.

The better questions are:

- Is the purpose transparent?
- Is the evidence appropriate for the claim?
- Are sources and methods visible?
- Is important context omitted?
- Does the writer distinguish fact, interpretation, and opinion?
- Is the source appropriate for the job I need it to perform?

An advocacy organization’s page may not be neutral, but it may be the best primary source for explaining that organization’s goals and language. It may be a weaker source for proving that all of its factual claims are correct. The source type and the research job must fit.

### Read the Result Page, Too

Lateral reading begins before you click. Scan the result page. Compare domains, titles, dates, and descriptions. Search results can reveal that a claim has been covered by universities, government agencies, professional organizations, local news outlets, and fact-checkers. That landscape helps you choose where to begin.

This practice is sometimes called **click restraint**: pause long enough to choose a promising route instead of automatically entering the first result.

## VI. Credible Does Not Automatically Mean Useful

Writers often collapse two different questions into one:

1. **Is this source credible?**
2. **Is this source useful for my paper?**

They are related, but they are not identical.

### Credibility

Credibility asks whether the source is trustworthy enough for the claim you intend to make.

Consider:

- author or organizational expertise;
- publication context;
- quality and transparency of evidence;
- date and currency;
- accuracy and traceability;
- purpose and possible conflicts of interest;
- whether other reliable sources support, complicate, or challenge the claim.

### Usefulness

Usefulness asks whether the source provides something your argument needs.

A useful source might supply:

- a definition;
- historical background;
- a statistic;
- a concrete example;
- a primary text for analysis;
- a method or framework;
- expert explanation;
- a counterargument;
- a limitation;
- a different population or context;
- evidence that complicates your first assumption.

### The Credibility–Usefulness Grid

| | Useful for this paper | Not useful for this paper |
|---|---|---|
| **Credible** | Keep and identify its job | Set aside, even though it is a good source |
| **Not yet credible enough** | Investigate further or trace to stronger evidence | Do not spend more time unless your purpose changes |

A credible scholarly article about sleep patterns among retired adults may be excellent research but not useful for a paper focused on first-year college students. A current student newspaper story may not carry the authority of a peer-reviewed study, but it may provide a valuable campus example or lead to a policy document and interviews.

### The One-Sentence Job Test

Before keeping a source, complete this sentence:

> **I will use this source to ________________________________.**

Strong answers:

- define bedtime procrastination;
- provide current data on college-student sleep;
- explain how algorithmic feeds encourage continued viewing;
- offer a counterargument about student choice and self-regulation;
- analyze the platform’s own language about screen-time controls;
- provide a local example of a campus wellness campaign.

Weak answers:

- use it in my paper;
- get a quote;
- prove my point;
- because it is scholarly;
- because it was the first result.

A source without a job becomes decorative luggage. It makes the paper heavier without helping it travel.

## VII. Match the Source Type to the Job

Source categories are not a ranking from “bad” to “good.” They help you predict what kind of information a source may contain and how it might function.

### Scholarly Sources

Scholarly journal articles and academic books are written for researchers, students, or professionals in a field. They often include specialized vocabulary, methods, references, and detailed engagement with previous scholarship.

Use scholarly sources for:

- research findings;
- disciplinary theories and methods;
- literature reviews;
- detailed analysis;
- the history of an academic conversation.

Scholarly sources may be too narrow or technical when you are first learning the topic. A strong overview can help you build the vocabulary needed to understand them.

### Professional or Trade Sources

Professional publications are written for people working in a field. They may discuss practices, trends, case studies, regulations, and field-specific concerns.

Use them for:

- current professional practices;
- industry vocabulary;
- workplace perspectives;
- applied examples;
- emerging concerns that have not yet produced much scholarship.

### Credible Journalism

Journalism can provide current reporting, interviews, investigation, explanation, and public context.

Use it for:

- recent events;
- local examples;
- interviews with relevant people;
- public response;
- investigations;
- accessible explanations that lead you toward original evidence.

Distinguish reporting from editorials, columns, sponsored content, and press releases.

### Government and Institutional Sources

Government agencies, colleges, courts, and public institutions publish laws, policies, data, reports, definitions, and official records.

Use them for:

- laws and regulations;
- official policies;
- census or public-health data;
- institutional reports;
- original public documents.

An official source can accurately explain what an institution says or records. You may still need independent sources to evaluate the institution’s interpretation or performance.

### Advocacy Sources

Advocacy groups argue for causes, communities, or policies.

Use them for:

- understanding a group’s position;
- identifying its values and assumptions;
- studying campaign language;
- locating claims, testimonies, or proposed solutions that require independent checking.

Do not pretend an advocacy source has no purpose. Name the purpose and evaluate the evidence.

### Primary Sources

Primary sources are direct evidence from the person, event, organization, culture, or period you are studying. Examples include speeches, interviews, advertisements, posts, policies, survey data, photographs, meeting minutes, songs, and artifacts.

Use primary sources when your paper analyzes what someone said, made, did, recorded, or presented.

A primary source gives direct access. It does not automatically give an objective or complete account.

### Reference and Background Sources

Reference works, topic overviews, research guides, and encyclopedias help you learn basic facts, names, dates, vocabulary, and debates.

Use them to:

- enter an unfamiliar topic;
- identify keywords;
- locate major people, events, theories, or controversies;
- find citations leading to more specialized sources.

A background source may guide the research even when it does not appear in the final paper.

> **Key Insight:** “Scholarly” describes a kind of source. It does not describe every job a research paper needs completed.

## VIII. Keep a Source Log: Save the Trail While It Is Still Warm

A source log prevents the familiar research disaster in which you remember seeing the perfect statistic but cannot remember where it lived, who published it, or whether the quotation in your notes was copied exactly.

A useful source log records both **what the source is** and **what you plan to do with it**.

### Source-Log Template

**Author or organization:**  
**Title:**  
**Publication or container:**  
**Date:**  
**Stable URL, DOI, database permalink, or other locator:**  
**Source type:**  
**Search lane:**  
**Search terms that found it:**  
**One- or two-sentence summary:**  
**Important evidence or passages:**  
**Page, paragraph, section, or timestamp:**  
**Credibility notes:**  
**How I’ll use this source:**  
**Questions, limits, or claims to check:**  
**Draft MLA Works Cited entry:**

### Completed Example

**Author or organization:** Michael A. Caulfield  
**Title:** “Four Moves”  
**Publication or container:** *Web Literacy for Student Fact-Checkers*  
**Date:** 2017  
**Locator:** https://pressbooks.pub/webliteracy/chapter/four-strategies/  
**Source type:** Open educational resource; digital-literacy instruction  
**Search lane:** Open web  
**Search terms that found it:** `Mike Caulfield four moves lateral reading`  
**Summary:** Caulfield presents a practical set of online fact-checking moves: look for previous work, trace information upstream, read laterally, and circle back when a route fails. The page notes that the approach was later streamlined into SIFT.  
**Important evidence:** The source explains that readers should investigate what other sources say about an unfamiliar publication rather than relying only on the publication’s own presentation.  
**Location:** Section “Four Moves”  
**Credibility notes:** Caulfield is identified as the author of an open textbook on web literacy. The chapter is openly licensed and directly explains the framework associated with his work. Stanford’s Civic Online Reasoning materials independently emphasize similar lateral-reading questions.  
**How I’ll use this source:** Define lateral reading and explain why source evaluation sometimes requires leaving the original page.  
**Questions or limits:** The 2017 chapter uses the earlier “four moves” wording, so I should also note the later SIFT terminology accurately.  
**Draft MLA entry:** Caulfield, Michael A. “Four Moves.” *Web Literacy for Student Fact-Checkers*, 2017, Pressbooks, https://pressbooks.pub/webliteracy/chapter/four-strategies/. Accessed 16 July 2026.

The field **How I’ll use this source** is the engine of the log. Without it, the source log is a storage closet. With it, the log becomes an early outline.

### Keep Your Notes Honest

Use labels that separate:

- **direct quotation** from the source;
- **paraphrase** of the source;
- **summary** of the source;
- **your reaction or analysis**;
- **a question to investigate**.

Quotation marks in research notes are not decorative. They tell your future self which words belong to someone else.

## IX. MLA Begins During Research, Not the Night Before Submission

Citation problems usually begin long before the Works Cited page. They begin when a writer saves a quotation without a page number, copies a temporary database-session URL, forgets the author, or cannot tell whether the title in the browser tab belongs to the article or the larger website.

Save citation details while the source is open.

Depending on the source, record:

- author or organization;
- title of the specific article, chapter, video, report, post, or page;
- title of the larger container, such as a journal, newspaper, website, anthology, or database;
- other contributors;
- version or edition;
- volume and issue;
- publisher;
- publication date;
- page range;
- DOI, stable URL, or database permalink;
- access date when appropriate.

MLA builds Works Cited entries from **core elements**. A container is the larger whole that holds the source. For example, an article is contained in a journal, and a page may be contained in a website.

### Source-to-Citation Example

Suppose you use Caulfield’s chapter “Four Moves” from the open textbook *Web Literacy for Student Fact-Checkers*.

**Author:** Michael A. Caulfield  
**Title of source:** “Four Moves”  
**Title of container:** *Web Literacy for Student Fact-Checkers*  
**Publication date:** 2017  
**Platform or publisher:** Pressbooks  
**URL:** https://pressbooks.pub/webliteracy/chapter/four-strategies/  
**Access date:** 16 July 2026

A draft MLA entry is:

> Caulfield, Michael A. “Four Moves.” *Web Literacy for Student Fact-Checkers*, 2017, Pressbooks, https://pressbooks.pub/webliteracy/chapter/four-strategies/. Accessed 16 July 2026.

If your sentence names the author, the in-text citation may not need a parenthetical author reference:

> Caulfield describes lateral reading as investigating what other sources say about an unfamiliar publication.

If your sentence does not name the author, use the shortest information that directs the reader to the Works Cited entry:

> Lateral reading asks researchers to investigate a publication through outside sources rather than relying entirely on the publication’s self-description (Caulfield).

When a source has stable page numbers and you quote or paraphrase a specific passage, include the relevant page number. For an unpaginated webpage, do not invent a page number. Follow your instructor’s directions if a course requires paragraph or section markers.

### Citation Generators Are Drafting Tools

Database and Google Scholar citation buttons can save time, but their output should be treated as a draft. Check:

- capitalization;
- italics and quotation marks;
- missing authors or dates;
- duplicate publisher information;
- URLs and DOIs;
- whether the source title and container title are in the correct places;
- whether the citation matches the version you actually used.

A generator can arrange information. It cannot always understand the source correctly.

## X. Source Triage: Keep, Maybe, or Not for This Paper

Source evaluation is rarely a simple contest between “good” and “bad.” A better triage system asks whether a source is credible enough, useful enough, and appropriate for the paper’s purpose.

Use three categories:

- **Keep:** credible enough and clearly useful;
- **Maybe, investigate further:** potentially useful, but the source or claim needs more checking;
- **Not for this paper:** irrelevant, redundant, too weak for the intended claim, or better replaced by a stronger source.

### Practice Research Question

> **Should college instructors require handwritten notes, allow digital notes, or let students choose based on the task and their learning needs?**

The following source cards are fictionalized for practice. Evaluate the source description, not a real publication.

### Source A: Peer-Reviewed Laboratory Study

A 2014 peer-reviewed psychology study compares students who took longhand notes with students who typed notes during several controlled lectures. It reports differences in how much students copied word-for-word and how they performed on some conceptual questions. The sample consists mainly of students at selective four-year institutions.

**Possible decision:** Keep, but define its job carefully.

**Reasoning:** The study may provide important evidence about note-taking behavior under specific conditions. It does not automatically settle what every student should do in every course. The date, sample, task, accessibility needs, device use, and later research matter.

### Source B: Recent Systematic Review

A 2025 peer-reviewed review examines dozens of studies on digital and handwritten note-taking. It finds that outcomes vary according to distraction, note-taking strategy, subject matter, device use, review habits, and assessment type. The authors warn against treating the medium alone as the only cause.

**Possible decision:** Keep.

**Reasoning:** A review can help place individual studies into a wider research conversation. Its job might be to complicate an absolute claim and identify the conditions that affect learning.

### Source C: Note-Taking App Company Blog

A software company publishes “Ten Scientific Reasons Digital Notes Always Win.” The page has no named author, links to the company’s product throughout, and refers to “research” without linking to studies.

**Possible decision:** Not for proving that digital notes improve learning. Maybe keep as a primary source if analyzing marketing rhetoric.

**Reasoning:** The company has a direct commercial interest, and the evidence cannot be traced. The page could still be useful if the paper studies how technology companies market note-taking tools.

### Source D: College Disability-Services Guidance

A college accessibility office explains that some students use laptops, tablets, recording tools, speech-to-text software, or structured digital notes as approved accommodations. The page describes the institution’s procedures but does not claim that one note-taking method produces better learning for all students.

**Possible decision:** Keep.

**Reasoning:** This source may provide essential context about accessibility and the problems created by a universal ban. Its job differs from a learning-science study.

### Source E: Student Newspaper Survey

A campus newspaper surveys 250 students about their preferred note-taking method. Most respondents say they prefer digital notes, but the survey does not measure learning, grades, or retention.

**Possible decision:** Maybe.

**Reasoning:** The survey can show student preference at one campus. It cannot prove which method leads to better learning. Keep it only if student preference or campus practice matters to the argument.

### What the Exercise Reveals

These sources do not compete for one trophy. They answer different questions:

- What happens in a controlled study?
- What does the wider research suggest?
- How does a company market its product?
- What accessibility needs must a policy consider?
- What do students say they prefer?

A strong paper may use several source types because the argument contains several kinds of claims.

## XI. Build One Real Source-Log Entry

Choose one source for your current assignment. Do not wait until you have ten sources. Complete one careful entry now.

### Step 1: Identify the Source

Record the author or organization, title, publication, date, source type, and stable link.

### Step 2: Summarize Before Quoting

In one or two sentences, explain the source’s central purpose or finding in your own words. A source you cannot summarize is not ready to enter your draft.

### Step 3: Investigate Credibility

Write two or three notes about authorship, publication context, evidence, date, purpose, and what other sources say.

### Step 4: Give the Source a Job

Complete:

> I will use this source to ____________________________________________.

Then make the job more specific. “Provide evidence” is a start. “Provide evidence that late-night short-form video use is associated with delayed sleep among college students” is a plan.

### Step 5: Save the Exact Evidence

Copy a short quotation only when the exact wording matters. Otherwise, summarize or paraphrase. Record the page, paragraph, section heading, table, figure, or timestamp immediately.

### Step 6: Draft the MLA Entry

Use the MLA core elements and compare your draft with a reliable model. Do not leave an unlabeled URL squatting in your notes like a raccoon in the attic.

---

## XII. Research Checkpoint

Before you begin drafting, check the following:

- I have a focused research question rather than only a broad topic.
- I can name the main concepts and alternative keywords in my question.
- I searched in more than one lane when the question required different source types.
- I revised my search terms instead of repeating one weak search.
- I investigated unfamiliar sources outside their own pages.
- I looked for better coverage of important claims.
- I traced quotations, statistics, images, and research claims toward their original context.
- I can explain the difference between a source’s credibility and its usefulness.
- I can complete “I will use this source to…” for every source I plan to cite.
- My sources do not all repeat the same point.
- I saved citation details, page numbers, stable links, and clear labels for quotations and paraphrases.
- I know which questions remain unanswered and what I need to search for next.

Research is not finished because you have reached a required number of sources. It is ready to become writing when your sources help you understand the question, make a responsible claim, and show your reader how the evidence fits together.

> **Final Key Insight:** Good research is not collecting information until the paper looks crowded. It is building a trustworthy route from a meaningful question to a supportable argument.
