# ENG 1010 Chapter 12 Development Blueprint

## Working title

**Finding and Evaluating Sources: From Search Results to Useful Evidence**

The current preview file remains `1010/chapter-6.html` until the approved sequence is renamed during full deployment. In the student-facing sequence, this is Chapter 12.

## Purpose

This chapter should help a first-year writer answer four practical questions:

1. Where should I search?
2. How do I decide whether a source is credible?
3. How do I decide whether a credible source is actually useful for my paper?
4. How do I save enough information to find, understand, cite, and use the source later?

The chapter should move students from “I found something online” to “I can explain what this source is, why it deserves attention, and how it will function in my argument.”

## Student-facing learning goals

By the end of the chapter, students should be able to:

- choose a search lane that fits the assignment and research question;
- distinguish library databases, Google Scholar, and the open web;
- refine a broad topic into searchable terms and related keywords;
- use SIFT and lateral reading to investigate a source;
- identify author, publication, evidence, date, purpose, and relevant context;
- distinguish credibility from usefulness;
- recognize when a popular, professional, governmental, advocacy, or primary source may be more useful than a scholarly article;
- trace a quotation, statistic, image, or claim back toward its original context;
- keep a source log that records both citation information and a planned use;
- preserve the information needed for an MLA Works Cited entry and in-text citation.

## Proposed chapter structure

### I. Research is a route, not a single search box

Open with a familiar problem: a student enters an entire essay question into a search engine, receives millions of results, and cannot tell which source belongs in the paper.

Core distinction:

- a **topic** names the territory;
- a **research question** gives the search a direction;
- **keywords** create multiple routes into the question.

Include a compact topic-to-keywords example showing synonyms, narrower terms, broader terms, people, institutions, locations, and dates.

### II. The three-lane search method

Present the approved sequence as three lanes rather than a rigid ladder:

1. **Library databases** for curated academic, newspaper, magazine, reference, and professional sources available through the college library.
2. **Google Scholar** for locating scholarly conversations, citation trails, related research, and versions that may be available through the library or an author’s repository.
3. **The open web** for government information, organizations, current reporting, primary materials, public statements, datasets, policies, multimedia, and sources that help establish context.

Explain that strong research often moves between lanes. A database article may supply terminology for an open-web search; an open-web report may identify an expert whose scholarship can be found through Google Scholar.

### III. Search like a writer, not a slot machine

Teach a short repeatable search cycle:

1. Search with two or three meaningful terms.
2. Scan titles, subjects, abstracts, and publication details.
3. Collect useful vocabulary from promising results.
4. revise the search with narrower, broader, or alternative terms.
5. record the search terms that worked.

Include practical moves:

- quotation marks for an exact phrase;
- `AND` to require both ideas;
- `OR` for synonyms;
- date and publication filters;
- subject terms in databases;
- following “cited by,” references, and related articles without treating citation count as automatic proof of quality.

### IV. SIFT: pause before you trust or share

Introduce SIFT in plain language:

- **Stop:** What do I know about this source, and what is my purpose for using it?
- **Investigate the source:** Who created it? Where was it published? What expertise, interests, or institutional role matters?
- **Find better coverage:** What do other credible sources say about the same claim or event?
- **Trace claims, quotations, and media:** Can I get closer to the original study, document, dataset, speech, image, or context?

Use a low-temperature example such as a viral post claiming that a study proves handwritten notes are always better than laptops. The activity should show that the post, news summary, study, and later replication or criticism are different sources with different jobs.

### V. Lateral reading: leave the page to understand the page

Explain that close reading asks what a page says, while lateral reading temporarily leaves the page to investigate the source from the outside.

A short lateral-reading routine:

1. open a new tab;
2. search the author, organization, and publication;
3. examine independent descriptions rather than relying only on an “About” page;
4. look for expertise, funding, ownership, editorial standards, corrections, and relevant reputation;
5. return to the source with better context.

Stress that bias is not a magical disqualifier. Every source has a perspective or purpose. The question is whether the source is transparent, evidence-based, appropriately contextualized, and suitable for the writer’s purpose.

### VI. Credible does not automatically mean useful

Build the chapter around this essential distinction:

- **Credibility:** Is the source trustworthy enough for the claim I am making?
- **Usefulness:** Does the source give me information, evidence, context, a definition, an example, a method, or a perspective that my argument actually needs?

Use a usefulness checklist:

- What specific question does this source help me answer?
- What claim, example, definition, statistic, counterargument, or context might I use?
- Is the source too broad, too narrow, too old, too technical, or too distant from my actual topic?
- Does it add something different from the sources I already have?
- Can I explain how I will use it in one sentence?

Include the guiding sentence:

> A source belongs in the paper because it has a job, not because it was difficult to find.

### VII. Match the source type to the job

Clarify source categories without turning them into a hierarchy in which “scholarly” always wins:

- scholarly research for methods, findings, and disciplinary conversations;
- credible journalism for current events, interviews, investigation, and public context;
- government and institutional sources for laws, policies, public data, definitions, and official records;
- professional or trade sources for practices within a field;
- advocacy sources for understanding a group’s claims, values, campaigns, and evidence, while investigating those claims independently;
- primary sources for direct evidence such as speeches, advertisements, posts, policies, interviews, artifacts, and datasets;
- reference sources for orientation and vocabulary rather than carrying the central argument.

Emphasize assignment fit and rhetorical purpose.

### VIII. The source log: save the trail while it is still warm

The source log should include:

- author or organization;
- title;
- publication or container;
- date;
- stable URL, DOI, database permalink, or other locator;
- source type;
- search terms or route used to find it;
- one- or two-sentence summary;
- important evidence or passages with page or paragraph locations;
- credibility notes;
- **How I’ll use this source**;
- questions, limitations, or claims that still need checking;
- draft MLA Works Cited information.

The “How I’ll use this source” field is essential. It turns the log from a storage bin into a writing plan.

### IX. MLA begins during research, not the night before submission

Connect research habits to later source-integration chapters:

- save the author, title, container, publisher, date, page range, DOI or URL, and access date when needed;
- copy quotations accurately and mark them clearly as quotations in notes;
- record page numbers immediately;
- distinguish the source’s words from the student’s summary, paraphrase, questions, and reactions;
- preserve the stable link rather than a temporary database-session URL;
- explain that citation generators can provide a draft, but the writer must compare the result with an MLA model.

Include at least one fully explained source-to-Works-Cited example and one in-text citation example in the eventual chapter HTML.

### X. Practice: source triage

Students review several brief source cards for a specific research question and assign each source to:

- **Keep**
- **Maybe, investigate further**
- **Not for this paper**

The explanation matters more than the category. Each decision should identify credibility, usefulness, and intended job.

The examples should include a credible but irrelevant scholarly article, a useful government source, a current news report, a source that traces to stronger original evidence, and a visually polished page with unclear authorship or evidence.

### XI. Practice: build one source-log entry

Provide a guided source-log form with local browser saving. The activity should ask the student to complete one real entry for the current assignment rather than a generic sample only.

The output should be easy to copy into notes or download later if a future approved tool adds export functionality.

### XII. Research checkpoint

End with a compact checkpoint:

- I have a focused research question.
- I searched in more than one lane when appropriate.
- I investigated the source outside its own page.
- I traced important claims toward their original context.
- I can explain what each source will do in my paper.
- I saved citation details, page numbers, and stable links.
- My group of sources includes useful difference rather than fourteen versions of the same point.

## Proposed interactive elements

1. **Keyword Builder:** turns a topic and research question into related search-term groups without generating the student’s argument.
2. **Three-Lane Route Chooser:** helps the student choose an appropriate starting lane for several research needs.
3. **SIFT Walkthrough:** reveals each investigative step for one sample claim.
4. **Credible vs. Useful Sort:** demonstrates that those judgments answer different questions.
5. **Source Triage Cards:** Keep, Maybe, or Not for This Paper, with explanatory feedback.
6. **Source Log:** locally saves one or more entries and keeps “How I’ll use this source” prominent.

The chapter should remain usable without JavaScript. Interactive elements must have visible instructions and meaningful static content in print and no-script views.

## Tone and accessibility

- Address first-year community-college writers directly and respectfully.
- Avoid presenting research as a purity test or a hunt for perfectly unbiased sources.
- Use concrete examples before terminology.
- Keep paragraphs and sections scannable without thinning the content into generic bullet lists.
- Explain unfamiliar library and research language at the point of use.
- Preserve keyboard operation, visible focus, screen-reader labels, responsive layouts, dark mode, print output, and no-JavaScript access.

## Source and attribution plan

The completed chapter should be grounded in authoritative, current materials, including:

- the SIFT framework and lateral-reading scholarship;
- CT State library guidance where available and appropriate;
- official MLA guidance or a reliable institutional MLA model;
- library-database and Google Scholar documentation for search features;
- reputable writing-center and information-literacy resources;
- approved open educational composition sources when substantive material is adapted.

No source should be cited unless its content has been opened and checked. Adapted material must carry clear attribution and licensing information where required.
