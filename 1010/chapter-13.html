Finding and Evaluating Sources

Find credible material and decide what belongs in your paper.

  • Use this chapter to move from reading and noticing toward a stronger draft.
I. Sources Have Jobs

A source is useful when it helps your essay do something. It might provide background, define a key term, offer research, complicate your claim, or give expert context. A source is not useful simply because it appears in a search result.

Key Takeaway

Choose sources because they help your argument, not because you needed to meet a quota.

II. Where to Search
MoveHow to Use It
Library DatabasesBest for academic articles, reliable background, and sources your instructor expects in college writing.
Google ScholarUseful for scholarly articles and books, but some results may be behind paywalls.
Open WebUseful for organizations, government pages, journalism, data, and current examples. Requires careful credibility checks.

For Essay 3, start with your object of investigation. Search for the topic plus a context word: design, attention, algorithm, architecture, habit, advertising, campus, public space, platform, or behavior.

III. The SIFT Check
  • Stop: Before using the source, pause. What do you know about it?
  • Investigate: Who made it? What are their credentials, organization, or purpose?
  • Find better coverage: Can you find a more reliable source on the same topic?
  • Trace claims: If the source cites data, quotations, or studies, can you trace them back?
IV. Source Log
MoveHow to Use It
CitationRecord enough information to build a Works Cited entry later.
CredibilityWhy should a reader trust this source?
UseWill it provide background, evidence, a definition, a lens, or a complication?
ConnectionHow does it connect to your thesis or object of investigation?
PracticeFind one source: Write a two-sentence source log entry explaining why it is credible and how it may help your essay.
