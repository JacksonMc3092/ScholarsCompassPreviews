I. Feedback Is Raw Material

Feedback can feel overwhelming because it often arrives as many separate comments. A revision plan turns those comments into a short list of actions.

You do not need to fix everything at once. Start with the changes that will most improve the essay's argument and clarity.

II. Sort the Feedback
MoveHow to Use It
Global RevisionThesis, focus, organization, major missing evidence, or the overall argument.
Paragraph RevisionTopic sentences, evidence, explanation, transitions, or paragraph order.
Source RevisionQuotation, paraphrase, signal phrases, citations, or source explanation.
Sentence/Format RevisionGrammar, punctuation, MLA, title, spacing, and Works Cited details.
III. Build a Revision Plan
  1. List the feedback you received.
  2. Group similar comments together.
  3. Choose the two or three changes that matter most.
  4. Write when and how you will make those changes.
Revision priority: My body paragraphs summarize sources one at a time.
Plan: Reorganize two paragraphs around values: transparency and audience trust. Use Auslander in both paragraphs as the lens.
IV. What to Ask When Stuck
  • Which comment points to the biggest issue?
  • Which revision will help the thesis most?
  • Which paragraph is doing too much?
  • Where does the reader need more evidence?
  • What should I ask my instructor, peer reviewer, or Writing Center tutor?
