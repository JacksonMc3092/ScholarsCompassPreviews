I. MLA Document Format

MLA format helps readers recognize your work as college writing and locate your sources. Always follow your instructor's directions first, but these are the standard basics.

  • Use a readable 12-point font, such as Times New Roman, Arial, or Calibri.
  • Double-space the entire paper.
  • Use 1-inch margins.
  • Include your last name and page number in the upper-right header if required.
  • Place your name, instructor, course, and date in the upper-left corner of the first page.
  • Center the title. Do not bold, underline, italicize, or enlarge it unless your instructor asks.

II. In-Text Citation Examples

An in-text citation points readers to the matching Works Cited entry. If a source has page numbers, include the page number. If it does not, use the author's name in the sentence or citation.

MoveHow to Use It
Author in sentenceChayka argues that digital platforms help spread a familiar global aesthetic.
Author in parenthesesDigital platforms help spread a familiar global aesthetic (Chayka).
Page numberWallace describes this habit of attention as the "default setting" (Wallace 3).
Video timestampAround 0:18, the camera shifts from the product to the family scene, making the chips part of a memory rather than just a snack.

Note: For advertisements and videos, timestamps can help your reader find a moment, but your instructor may not require them in formal MLA citation. Use them when they make your evidence clearer.

IV. Common MLA Problems to Check
  • The paper is not double-spaced.
  • The Works Cited page is missing or includes sources not used in the paper.
  • In-text citations do not match Works Cited entries.
  • Article, video, or webpage titles are missing quotation marks.
  • Website or container titles are not italicized.
  • Quoted material appears without a signal phrase or explanation.
PracticeMLA check: Choose one quotation or paraphrase from your draft. Find the matching Works Cited entry. If you cannot connect them, fix the citation before submitting.
