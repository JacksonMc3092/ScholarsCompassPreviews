I. Observation Is Evidence

Essay 3 asks you to read the world around you. That means your own observations matter. A screenshot, sign, seating arrangement, recommendation pattern, repeated phrase, hallway layout, app notification, or routine can become evidence if you describe it carefully and explain what it reveals.

The goal is not to collect random details. The goal is to build a record of patterns that help you make a claim.

II. What Counts as an Artifact?
  • Photos or sketches of a space
  • Screenshots of a feed, app, recommendation, search result, or interface
  • Signs, rules, menus, labels, captions, or repeated phrases
  • Notes from observing a routine, commute, workplace, campus space, store, or gym
  • Patterns in what gets recommended, centered, hidden, repeated, or discouraged

Important: Protect privacy. Do not photograph people in private or vulnerable situations. If you need to describe behavior, use general observation notes rather than identifying individuals.

III. Evidence Log Template
MoveHow to Use It
ObservationWhat did you see, hear, read, or experience?
ContextWhere did it happen? When? Under what conditions?
PatternIs this isolated, repeated, designed, required, encouraged, or hidden?
MeaningWhat might it teach people to notice, value, do, avoid, or accept as normal?
Source ConnectionWhich course reading or outside source might help explain it?
Example: A public bench has metal dividers that prevent lying down. This is not just a seating choice; it suggests the space is designed for brief sitting, not rest or shelter.
IV. From Log to Paragraph

A log entry becomes a paragraph when you turn the observation into a claim.

Observation: My gym places mirrors across the free-weight area.
Paragraph claim: The mirror layout turns exercise into a visible, measurable, and comparative activity, which can make discipline feel public rather than private.
