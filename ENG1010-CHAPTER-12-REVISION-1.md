### A Rhetorical Reminder: Ethos, Logos, and Pathos

The rhetorical appeals you encountered earlier can help you examine a source, though they should not become a three-box scoring system.

**Ethos** asks how the source establishes credibility. Consider the author’s expertise, experience, institutional role, fairness, transparency, and publication context. A source may build ethos through relevant credentials, careful citation, acknowledgment of limitations, or a record of accurate reporting. A polished design, impressive job title, or confident tone may create the appearance of ethos without providing much reason for trust.

**Logos** asks how the source supports its claims. Look for evidence, examples, data, methods, definitions, reasoning, and connections between claims. Ask whether the evidence actually supports the conclusion and whether important qualifications have disappeared. A source can contain statistics and still use them poorly. Numbers are evidence only when readers can understand where they came from and what they mean.

**Pathos** asks how emotion shapes the audience’s response. Emotion is not automatically dishonest. A firsthand story about housing insecurity, disability access, or workplace injury may help readers understand the human stakes of an issue. The question is whether emotional language or imagery deepens the evidence or tries to replace it.

These appeals often work together. A public-health report may build ethos through expert authorship, logos through transparent data, and pathos through stories that show why the findings matter. Your task is not to count the appeals. Your task is to explain how the source earns trust, supports its claims, and directs the audience’s attention.

> **Key Insight:** Rhetorical effectiveness and factual reliability overlap, but they are not identical. A source can sound highly persuasive and still require investigation.

## VII. Academic, Popular, and Professional Sources: Know the Conversation You Are Entering

Source categories are not a ranking from “bad” to “good.” They tell you something about a source’s audience, purpose, writing style, review process, and likely uses. The same topic may appear in a peer-reviewed journal, a newspaper, a professional magazine, a government report, and an advocacy website. Those sources are participating in different conversations and doing different jobs.

### Academic and Scholarly Sources

**Academic** or **scholarly sources** are produced within colleges, universities, research institutions, or scholarly fields. They include peer-reviewed journal articles, academic books, book chapters, literature reviews, conference papers, dissertations, and some research reports.

They are usually written by scholars or researchers for readers who already have some knowledge of the field. Common features include:

- specialized vocabulary;
- an abstract or formal introduction;
- engagement with previous research;
- a methods section when original research is involved;
- extensive citations or references;
- careful qualification of claims;
- publication through an academic journal, university press, scholarly society, or research institution.

Academic sources are especially useful for research findings, disciplinary theories, methods, detailed analysis, and understanding the longer scholarly conversation around a question.

### Peer-Reviewed Sources

A **peer-reviewed source** is a particular kind of academic source. Before publication, specialists in the same or a closely related field evaluate the work. They may examine the research question, methods, evidence, reasoning, use of previous scholarship, and significance of the findings. The author may be asked to revise the article before it is accepted.

Peer review is a form of scholarly quality control. It does not guarantee that a source is flawless, unbiased, current, or useful for your paper. Peer-reviewed studies can use limited samples, make mistakes, disagree with one another, or later be challenged by new evidence. Peer review tells you something important about how the source entered the academic conversation, not that the conversation has ended.

Also remember that **scholarly** and **peer-reviewed** are not perfect synonyms. An academic book from a university press may be scholarly even though it did not pass through the same journal-review process. A preprint may present serious academic research before formal peer review. A conference paper or dissertation may also be scholarly but require additional checking.

> **Key Insight:** “Peer-reviewed” describes a review process. It does not mean “automatically correct,” and it does not tell you whether the source fits your particular question.

### Popular Sources

**Popular sources** are written for a broad public audience. They include newspapers, general-interest magazines, news websites, podcasts, documentaries, and many widely read online publications.

Popular sources often:

- use accessible language;
- explain unfamiliar concepts rather than assuming disciplinary knowledge;
- focus on current events, public interest, people, or practical consequences;
- use shorter articles and fewer formal citations;
- rely on journalists, editors, interviews, links, or named sources rather than an academic reference list;
- include images, headlines, storytelling, and other features designed for public readers.

Popular does not mean unreliable. Strong journalism can provide timely reporting, local information, interviews, investigation, public response, and clear explanations of complex research. A reputable newspaper may be the best source for an event that happened yesterday. However, students should distinguish reporting from editorials, opinion columns, sponsored content, press releases, and entertainment.

A popular article that summarizes a study may help you understand the issue, but trace important research claims back to the original study whenever possible. The news article and the study may both be useful, but they perform different jobs.

### Trade or Professional Sources

**Trade** or **professional sources** are written for people who work in a particular occupation, industry, or professional field. Examples include publications for nurses, teachers, attorneys, engineers, architects, marketers, librarians, criminal-justice professionals, or hospitality managers.

These sources often:

- assume knowledge of the profession;
- use field-specific vocabulary;
- discuss current practices, regulations, technologies, products, or workplace problems;
- emphasize practical application rather than building an academic theory;
- include case studies, interviews, professional commentary, industry data, or reports from conferences;
- carry advertising connected to the profession or industry.

Trade and professional sources can be excellent for understanding how people in a field describe their work, what problems practitioners face, and how new policies or technologies are being applied. They may be less useful for proving a broad causal claim unless their evidence and methods are clear.

### A Quick Comparison

| Feature | Peer-reviewed academic | Other scholarly or academic | Popular | Trade or professional |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary audience** | researchers and specialists | scholars, students, and specialists | general public | practitioners in a field |
| **Typical author** | researcher or scholar | scholar, academic expert, or research institution | journalist, editor, expert contributor, or public writer | practitioner, industry expert, editor, or professional organization |
| **Review process** | evaluated by disciplinary peers before publication | varies by format and publisher | editorial review, fact-checking, or platform-specific review | professional or editorial review; sometimes sponsored or industry supported |
| **Evidence style** | methods, data, citations, literature review | citations, scholarly analysis, archival or theoretical evidence | interviews, links, public documents, observation, summarized research | case studies, workplace data, regulations, professional experience, industry reports |
| **Language** | specialized and formal | often specialized and formal | accessible to broad readers | field-specific and practical |
| **Strongest uses** | original research, methods, disciplinary findings | theory, history, detailed analysis, scholarly context | current events, public context, interviews, local examples, accessible explanation | current practice, professional trends, applied examples, industry language |
| **Questions to ask** | Is the study current, well designed, and relevant to my population and claim? | What scholarly process or publisher supports it, and how does it fit the field? | Is this reporting, opinion, sponsored content, or summary? Can I trace major claims? | Who supports the publication? Is the evidence independent, transparent, and appropriate for my claim? |

### How the Rhetorical Appeals Appear Across Source Types

Different source types often build ethos, logos, and pathos in different ways.

- A peer-reviewed article may build **ethos** through the author’s disciplinary expertise and the journal’s review process. Its **logos** may appear through methods, evidence, and qualified conclusions. Pathos is usually restrained, though the choice of problem can still signal urgency or values.
- A popular news feature may build **ethos** through a respected publication, named reporters, interviews, corrections, and links to evidence. It may use narrative and images to create **pathos**, while its **logos** comes from documentation and explanation.
- A professional publication may build **ethos** through practical experience and familiarity with a field. Its **logos** may rely on case studies, regulations, performance data, or workplace examples. It may also appeal to professional values such as efficiency, safety, service, or innovation.

Do not assume that formal language equals strong ethos or that emotional language equals weak evidence. Examine what the source actually does.

### Choosing Among These Sources

Match the source to the claim.

- To explain the results of a controlled study, use the study or a strong scholarly review.
- To describe how a new policy is affecting local residents, current journalism may be more useful.
- To explain how professionals are applying a new technology, a trade publication may provide the clearest practical evidence.
- To establish official rules or statistics, use the relevant government or institutional source.
- To analyze how a company presents itself, the company’s own advertisement or public statement may be the correct primary source.

A strong research paper often combines source types. The paper becomes stronger when the writer understands what each source can establish and does not ask one source to perform a job it cannot support.

> **Key Insight:** Source type shapes the kind of ethos, evidence, language, and audience a source is likely to use. Identify the type, then decide whether it fits the job your argument needs completed.

- peer-reviewed journal article;
- scholarly book chapter;
- popular news report;
- newspaper editorial;
- trade-magazine article;

### Source triage exercise

Add this question to each source card:

> What kind of ethos does this source claim, and what evidence or logos supports that credibility?

Students should also notice when emotionally powerful language or personal testimony supplies useful pathos but does not, by itself, establish a broad factual claim.
